Shutter Speed

Shutter speed is how long your camera's sensor is exposed to light. A fast shutter freezes motion; a slow shutter lets movement blur across the frame in long, painterly streaks.

Next: ISO →

Key Concepts

Fast Shutter 1/500s and above freezes fast-moving subjects — sports, wildlife, splashing water. Lets in less light, so pair with wide aperture or higher ISO.
Slow Shutter 1/30s and below creates motion blur. Waterfalls turn silky, car headlights become light trails, and night scenes become possible.
Handheld Limit A common rule: don't shoot slower than 1/(focal length). On a 50mm lens, avoid going slower than 1/50s without a tripod or image stabilisation.
Exposure Impact Shutter speed directly controls how much total light hits the sensor. One full stop doubles or halves the exposure time and thus the light.

Practice & Reference

Common uses by speed

  • 1/2000s+ — birds in flight, motorsport
  • 1/500s – 1/1000s — sports, children running
  • 1/125s – 1/250s — everyday handheld shooting
  • 1/30s – 1s — low light, waterfalls, panning
  • 1s+ — light trails, star trails, night sky

Practice exercises

  1. Photograph running water at 1/1000s and 1/2s — compare the results side by side.
  2. At night, set a 10–20s exposure and wave a torch for light painting.
  3. Try panning: track a moving car at 1/30s for a blurred background with a sharp subject.
