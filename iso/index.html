Chapter 3

ISO

ISO controls how sensitive your camera's sensor is to light. Raising ISO brightens the image, but at the cost of noise — grain and colour speckling that can ruin fine detail.

Back to Aperture

Key Concepts

Low ISO (100–400) Clean, noise-free images. Use whenever you have enough light — outdoors in daylight, in a well-lit studio, or with a tripod.
Mid ISO (800–3200) Slight noise but still very usable on modern cameras. Good for indoor events, overcast days, and concerts without flash.
High ISO (6400+) Visible noise but sometimes necessary — night photography, dimly lit venues, handheld astrophotography. Noise reduction in post can help.
Native ISO Every sensor has a "native" or "base" ISO — typically 100 or 200 — where it performs cleanest. Stay close to it when image quality is critical.

Practice & Reference

ISO quick reference

  • ISO 100 — bright sunlight, studio with flash
  • ISO 400 — overcast, open shade
  • ISO 800–1600 — indoors, golden hour
  • ISO 3200+ — night, candle-lit scenes
  • ISO 12800+ — very dark, accept noise

Practice exercises

  1. In a dim room, shoot the same scene at ISO 400, 1600, and 6400. Examine noise at 100% zoom.
  2. Find your camera's highest usable ISO — where noise becomes unacceptable to you.
  3. Shoot in Auto ISO with a minimum shutter speed set to see how your camera balances the two.

Putting It All Together

Now that you understand aperture, shutter speed, and ISO individually, the real skill is balancing all three for any given scene.

In bright light, use a low ISO, small aperture, and fast shutter. In dim light, open the aperture, slow the shutter, and raise the ISO — in that order — until you get the exposure you need.

  • Start with ISO

    Set it as low as your conditions allow. Only raise it when aperture and shutter speed have been pushed to their limits.

  • Then set aperture

    Choose the depth of field you want. This constrains how much light the lens can gather.

  • Finally, shutter speed

    Adjust to nail the exposure. Fast enough to avoid camera shake or freeze motion; slow enough to let in sufficient light.
