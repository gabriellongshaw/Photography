Chapter 1

Aperture

Aperture is the opening inside your lens that controls how much light reaches the sensor. Measured in f-stops, it also determines how much of your scene appears in sharp focus.

Next: Shutter Speed →

Key Concepts

Wide Aperture (low f-number) f/1.4 – f/2.8 lets in a lot of light and produces a shallow depth of field. The background blurs beautifully, isolating your subject.
Narrow Aperture (high f-number) f/8 – f/22 lets in less light but keeps more of the scene in sharp focus — ideal for landscapes and architecture.
Depth of Field The range of distance that appears acceptably sharp. Wide apertures give shallow depth of field; narrow apertures give deep depth of field.
Exposure Impact Aperture is one side of the exposure triangle. Opening up by one stop doubles the light; closing down by one stop halves it.

Practice & Reference

Common f-stop uses

  • f/1.4 – f/2.8 — portraits, low-light, subject isolation
  • f/4 – f/5.6 — everyday, travel, general use
  • f/8 – f/11 — landscapes, architecture, maximum sharpness
  • f/16 – f/22 — full-scene focus, sunstars

Practice exercises

  1. Shoot a small object at f/1.8, f/5.6, and f/16. Compare background blur.
  2. Use Aperture Priority (A / Av) mode to control f-stop while the camera handles shutter speed.
  3. Move closer to your subject at the same aperture to further reduce depth of field.
